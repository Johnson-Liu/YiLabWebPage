Research Interests


  • Functional optical imaging

  • Fiber optics/endoscopy

  • Superresolution microscopy

  • Translational research

A basic purpose of imaging is to visualize structural details in cell/tissue/organ. Beyond structure, seeing the function in a biological system provides valuable information.

Endoscopic imaging using fiber optics delivers and collects light through digestive tract or open airways. The small size limit poses a grant challenge to integrate sophisticated optical system into an endoscopic body or existing endoscopy working channel.

Abby’s diffraction limit has governed the smallest feature that is resolvable by optical microscopy to about 0.5 microns, for more than one century. Superresolution microscopy is to break the diffraction limit and achieve nanoscale resolution.

One major focus of this lab is to translate the development of novel optical technique to clinics, and ultimately benefit the general public.

BIOGRAPHY

His work focuses on novel optical techniques for early disease detection, and monitoring disease progression and prognosis. Among other inventions, he developed various imaging methods that enables noninvasive detection of nanoscale structural alterations in tissue and the local tissue metabolism. By applying those techniques, he demonstrated the detection of the earliest form of colorectal and pancreatic cancers, colonization of metastatic breast cancer cells, and blinding retinal diseases such as diabetic retinopathy. He also studied the nanoscale structural changes in both cellular and extracellular components in field carcinogenesis. His research is at the interface of biophotonic, physics, engineering, biology and medicine, that ultimately aim to improve the health care of general public and our community.

RESEARCH INTERESTS

Biophotonics, super-resolution imaging, fiber optics and endoscopy, structural and functional relation in biological systems, Early detection and monitor of cancer and other chronic diseases, optical biomarkers.
